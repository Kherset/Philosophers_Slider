Aristotle
Greek philosopher and polymath, studied under Plato and taught Alexander the Great. Known for his empirical approach, he sought to systematize knowledge across disciplines, from biology to ethics. His works emphasize logic, virtue, and the pursuit of eudaimonia (flourishing). Aristotle's legacy profoundly influenced science, philosophy, and education for centuries.

Epictetus
Greek Stoic philosopher and former slave, taught that true freedom lies in mastering one’s inner responses, not external circumstances. His Discourses and Handbook emphasize the power of reason, acceptance of fate, and living in accordance with nature. Epictetus’s practical wisdom continues to inspire resilience and self-mastery.

Epicurus
Greek philosopher and founder of Epicureanism, taught that the pursuit of happiness lies in simple pleasures, friendship, and freedom from fear. He rejected superstition and emphasized the role of reason in achieving ataraxia (tranquility). Though often misunderstood, his philosophy promotes a balanced, thoughtful life.

Marcus Aurelius
Roman emperor and Stoic philosopher, is renowned for his Meditations, written during military campaigns. Drawing from Stoicism, he emphasized self-discipline, acceptance of fate, and living virtuously. Despite the challenges of ruling an empire, he sought to align personal ethics with public duties. His reign, marked by wars and crises, remains a model of integrating philosophy into leadership. His writings continue to inspire those pursuing wisdom and resilience in the face of adversity

Plato
Greek philosopher and founder of the Academy, is renowned for his dialogues exploring justice, knowledge, and the nature of reality. A student of Socrates and teacher of Aristotle, he developed the Theory of Forms, asserting the existence of eternal truths beyond the physical world. His work, blending metaphysics, ethics, and politics, continues to shape Western thought.

Seneca
Roman statesman and Stoic philosopher, blended practical ethics with reflections on power and mortality. His writings, such as Letters to Lucilius, stress self-control, resilience, and the fleeting nature of life. Despite the complexities of his political career, Seneca’s philosophy endures as a guide to navigating adversity with virtue.

Socrates
Greek philosopher, is famed for his method of questioning to seek truth and virtue. He left no writings, but his teachings, recorded by Plato, championed self-examination and the pursuit of wisdom. Executed for "corrupting the youth," Socrates remains a symbol of intellectual integrity and the fearless search for ethical understanding.
